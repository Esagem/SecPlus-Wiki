# 1.2 — Fundamental Security Concepts

CompTIA description: *"Summarize fundamental security concepts."*

This objective is a grab-bag of the vocabulary the rest of the exam leans on — the CIA triad, non-repudiation, AAA, gap analysis, zero trust, physical security, and deception. No unifying matrix; just seven self-contained concept clusters. Master the distinctions inside each cluster and you've got it.

---

## CIA Triad

The three security objectives every control ultimately serves.

| Letter | Goal | Achieved by |
|---|---|---|
| **C**onfidentiality | Keep information away from people who shouldn't see it | Encryption, access controls, MFA |
| **I**ntegrity | Ensure data wasn't altered between sender and receiver | Hashing, digital signatures, certificates |
| **A**vailability | Keep systems and data reachable when needed | Redundancy, fault tolerance, patching |

Sometimes called the **AIC Triad** to avoid confusion with the US Central Intelligence Agency — same three letters, same meaning.

**Trap:** Encryption alone is a confidentiality control, not integrity. You can encrypt data and still let a bit-flip corrupt it; the recipient won't notice without a hash or signature.

---

## Non-repudiation

You can't credibly deny that you sent something. Two ingredients:

- **Proof of integrity** — the data received is bit-identical to the data sent. Provided by a **hash** (a.k.a. message digest / fingerprint).
- **Proof of origin** — the sender's identity can be verified by anyone. Provided by a **digital signature**, which is a hash encrypted with the sender's private key.

Hash alone gives you integrity but not origin: you know nothing was changed, but not who sent it. Signature gives you both, because only the holder of the private key could have produced it, and anyone with the matching public key can verify.

**Trap:** Encryption ≠ signature. Encrypting a message with the recipient's public key gives confidentiality, not non-repudiation. To sign, the *sender* encrypts a hash with the *sender's private key*.

## Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting (AAA)

The three-stage login lifecycle.

| Stage | Question answered | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Identification** | Who are you claiming to be? | Username |
| **Authentication** | Prove it | Password, MFA, biometric, certificate |
| **Authorization** | What are you allowed to access? | Permissions, groups, roles |
| **Accounting** | What did you do? | Logs of login, data sent/received, logout |

Identification is sometimes folded into authentication. AAA = the three "A" stages.

### AAA for systems (not users)

People prove identity by knowing a password. A laptop or server can't type one — and you don't want passwords stored on devices in the field anyway. The fix: install a **digitally signed certificate** on the device, issued by your **Certificate Authority (CA)**. During authentication, the system presents the cert; the verifier confirms the CA's signature using the CA's public certificate. Same trust chain a browser uses for HTTPS, applied to device authentication.

### Authorization models

The point of an authorization model is to scale. Without one, you'd grant rights user-by-user, resource-by-resource — unmanageable past a handful of people. The model is the **abstraction** between users and resources: roles, groups, attributes, organizational position. Add one user to the "Shipping" group and they inherit every permission attached to it. Detailed model types live in [[objectives/4.6|objective 4.6]].

**Trap:** "AAA server" on the exam is almost always the central server (RADIUS / TACACS+ / Kerberos KDC) — the VPN concentrator or wireless controller just *forwards* credentials. The concentrator doesn't store passwords; the AAA server does.

## Gap analysis

A formal study of *where you are* versus *where you need to be*, used to drive a security roadmap.

The process:

1. Pick a **baseline** — NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2 (Controlled Unclassified Information), ISO/IEC 27001 (information security management systems), or one you author yourself.
2. Evaluate **people** (training, formal experience, policy awareness) and **processes** (existing systems vs. policy).
3. Break broad control families into individual sub-controls, score each location/system against them.
4. Produce a **report** with red/yellow/green status across sites or controls, and a path (cost, time, equipment, change-control work) to close the gaps.

**Trap:** Gap analysis is *not* a one-time deliverable — it takes weeks to months and gets re-run periodically. If a scenario describes a "snapshot of current vs. desired security posture with remediation paths," that's gap analysis, not risk assessment (which focuses on threats and likelihood) and not an audit (which checks compliance against a fixed standard).

## Zero Trust

The model: **never assume any subject, device, or request is trustworthy** — verify every access, every time. Replaces the old "hard shell, soft interior" perimeter design where anyone past the firewall could roam freely.

### Data plane vs. control plane

Every networking or security device can be split into two functional planes:

| Plane | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Data plane** | Moves actual traffic — frames, packets, sessions | Forwarding, NAT, encryption-in-transit |
| **Control plane** | Configures and decides what the data plane should do | Routing tables, firewall rules, policy definitions |

Same split applies to physical, virtual, and cloud devices.

### Adaptive identity & policy-driven access

Authentication looks at more than the password. **Adaptive identity** factors in:

- Source IP and geography (a US user's account hitting from China — flag).
- Relationship to the org (full-time, contractor, departed).
- Connection type (corporate VPN, home Wi-Fi, public network).
- Time of day, device posture.

All of those feed a **policy-driven access control** decision that strengthens or denies authentication dynamically.

### Security zones & implicit trust

Connections classified by zone: trusted ↔ untrusted, internal ↔ external. Rules govern which zone can reach which. Inside a defined zone, **implicit trust** can apply — e.g., "anything from the trusted-corporate zone to the internal data-center zone is permitted without re-auth."

### The four control-plane components (memorize)

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| **Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)** | The gatekeeper. Every request flows through it. Gathers info; does *not* decide. |
| **Policy Decision Point (PDP)** | Decides allow/deny. Consists of the Policy Engine + Policy Administrator. |
| **Policy Engine** | Evaluates the request against predefined policies. Grants, denies, revokes. |
| **Policy Administrator** | Generates access tokens/credentials based on the engine's decision and passes them to the PEP. |

Flow: subject → PEP gathers info → PDP (engine decides, administrator issues token) → PEP enforces.

**Trap:** The PEP does not make the decision. It only *enforces* what the PDP returned. Exam questions love to swap these.

## Physical Security

Tangible controls protecting people, equipment, and access to spaces.

| Control | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Bollards / barricades** | Channel pedestrians, block vehicles | Often visually obvious — partly deterrent |
| **Access control vestibule (mantrap)** | One door at a time; controls flow through a transition | Three patterns: both unlock unless one opens · one always locked while other open · normally locked, badge to unlock first |
| **Fences** | Perimeter boundary; transparent or opaque, often with razor wire | Robustness matters more than visibility |
| **Cameras / CCTV** | Recording and detection; can include motion detection, object recognition, face/license-plate reading | Networked, centrally stored |
| **Security guards** | Live human verification at entry points | Often paired in twos for **two-person integrity** — no single guard can bypass controls alone |
| **Access badges** | ID + electronic lock integration, logged centrally | Photo + name visible at all times |
| **Lighting** | More light = harder to sneak; angle matters for camera-based facial recognition | |
| **Sensors** | Infrared (motion in a small area) · Microwave (motion over a larger area) · Ultrasonic (motion + collision via sound reflection) · Pressure (force sensing in a defined zone) | |

**Trap:** A camera is *detective* (records events) by control type, *physical* by category, and *deterrent* when the camera is obviously visible. Same device, different role depending on what the question highlights — see [[objectives/1.1|objective 1.1]] for the type/category split.

## Deception and Disruption

Controls that waste attacker time, gather intelligence, and detect intrusion by attractiveness rather than blocking.

| Control | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Honeypot** | A fake system designed to attract attackers. Watch what tools and techniques get used against it. |
| **Honeynet** | A network of honeypots — fake workstations, servers, routers, firewalls — wired together to look like a real environment. More believable, holds attention longer. |
| **Honeyfile** | A bait file (e.g., `passwords.txt`) on a real system. Should never be touched in normal operation. Access triggers an alert. |
| **Honeytoken** | Traceable fake data seeded in the wild — fake API keys, fake email addresses, fake DB records, fake cookies. If they surface elsewhere (a paste site, an attacker's tool, a third-party breach), you know your environment leaked. |

The "race" — attackers improve at distinguishing honeypots from real systems; defenders make honeypots more realistic. See `projecthoneypot.org` for community work.

**Trap:** Honey* controls are **deceptive** rather than preventive. They don't stop the attacker; they expose them. On 1.1's type axis they are best classified as **detective** (and sometimes deterrent if the attacker knows you use them).

## How to read scenario questions

For 1.2 vocabulary questions, the trick is matching the scenario's *active verb* to the right concept:

- "Verifies data wasn't altered" → integrity → hash.
- "Verifies who sent it, anyone can check" → non-repudiation → digital signature.
- "Verifies the data is hidden from outsiders" → confidentiality → encryption.
- "Always check, never trust the network position" → zero trust.
- "Compare current state to a target" → gap analysis.
- "Decides allow/deny" → PDP. "Enforces" → PEP.
- "Fake resource to attract an attacker" → honeypot (or honeynet if it's a whole environment).
