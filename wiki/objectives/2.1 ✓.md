# 2.1 — Threat Actors

CompTIA description: *"Compare and contrast common threat actors and motivations."*

A threat actor is the entity behind an event that affects security. The objective is to recognize each archetype by its attributes — where it sits relative to your org, what it has to spend, how skilled it is, and what it actually wants. Get those four right and you can classify any scenario CompTIA throws at you.

---

## The four attribute axes

Every threat actor is described along the same axes. The exam constantly mixes-and-matches them.

| Axis | What it asks | Values |
|---|---|---|
| **Location** | Where does the actor sit relative to the org? | Internal · External |
| **Resources / Funding** | What can they bring to bear? | Limited · Moderate · Extensive |
| **Sophistication / Capability** | How deep do their skills go? | Low (script-running) · Medium · High (custom tooling, zero-days) |
| **Motivation** | Why are they doing it? | Data exfiltration · Espionage · Service disruption · Philosophical/political · Revenge · Financial gain · War · Ethical |

A control rarely classifies in isolation — what makes a scenario question hard is that two actors can share a motivation (financial gain works for both organized crime and a vengeful insider) but differ on the other three axes.

---

## The threat actor matrix

|  | Location | Resources | Sophistication | Typical motivation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nation state** | External | Extensive | High | Espionage, disruption, war |
| **Unskilled attacker** | Usually external | Limited | Low | Disruption, data exfil, philosophical |
| **Hacktivist** | Usually external | Limited (some crowd-funded) | High | Philosophical / political |
| **Insider threat** | Internal | Already inside (org's own) | Medium — but knows where the data is | Revenge, financial gain |
| **Organized crime** | External | Extensive | High | Financial gain |
| **Shadow IT** | Internal | Limited (departmental budget) | Low–medium | Convenience (not malice) |

Each row is a starting point, not a hard rule — an insider with sysadmin skills can be sophisticated; a nation-state campaign can use unskilled mercenaries for routine work. But these are the canonical exam answers.

## The six threat actors

### Nation state
A government or an arm of a government (often the national-security wing) conducting cyber operations. The reference example for **APT** — Advanced Persistent Threat — because they have the funding to sit on a network for months or years using custom-built tools. Stuxnet (US/Israel, designed to destroy Iranian nuclear centrifuges) is the canonical case study.

- **Resources:** essentially unlimited; can run parallel campaigns.
- **Sophistication:** the highest tier. Custom malware, zero-days, supply-chain implants.
- **Motivations:** espionage, data exfiltration, disrupting another government's services, pulling a target into a war, philosophical/political ends.
- **Typical targets:** military command, utilities, financial systems, other governments.

**Exam trap:** "APT" is not a separate actor — it's a label for the *style* of attack that nation-states (and sometimes top-tier organized crime) run. If a question mentions persistence over many months with custom tooling and a state-level target, the answer is nation state.

### Unskilled attacker
Often called a "script kiddie" in the wild, but CompTIA prefers "unskilled attacker." Runs publicly available scripts and tools without understanding the underlying mechanics. If the script works, they win; if it doesn't, they can't troubleshoot.

- **Resources:** very limited. No organizational backing.
- **Sophistication:** low. Cannot modify or develop their own tooling.
- **Motivations:** the attack itself, sometimes data exfil or disruption, occasionally philosophical.
- **Location:** usually external, but occasionally found internally.

**Exam trap:** unskilled ≠ harmless. They use the easiest available path — default credentials, unpatched systems, leaked databases. Routine hygiene defeats most of them, which is exactly why CompTIA emphasizes patching and password policy.

### Hacktivist
A hacker motivated by a political, social, or philosophical cause. Despite "activist" framing, hacktivists are often **very technically sophisticated** — that's what separates them from the unskilled attacker.

- **Resources:** typically limited, though some operate via crowdfunding.
- **Sophistication:** high. They write their own tooling and target specific orgs surgically.
- **Motivations:** philosophical, political, social-justice ends. Goals are publicity, embarrassment, or disruption rather than money.
- **Typical actions:** denial-of-service, website defacement, leaking private documents.
- **Location:** usually external; sometimes they get hired in to become an insider.

**Exam trap:** distinguish from unskilled by *sophistication* and from organized crime by *motivation*. A defacement of a government website to protest a policy is hacktivist; the same defacement for ransom is organized crime.

### Insider threat
Someone with legitimate access who turns malicious — for revenge, financial gain, or coercion. The hardest type to detect because they're already inside every security boundary.

- **Resources:** already has the org's resources.
- **Sophistication:** medium technically, but **near-perfect knowledge of where the data lives** and which controls exist to circumvent.
- **Motivations:** revenge (often post-termination or post-grievance), financial gain, sometimes external coercion.
- **Detection:** hardest of all archetypes. Behavior analytics, separation of duties, least privilege, and background checks during hiring are the controls that matter.

**Exam trap:** an insider isn't always a sysadmin. A disgruntled accountant who emails the customer list to a competitor is an insider threat. The defining trait is **legitimate access being abused**, not technical skill.

### Organized crime
A criminal enterprise that runs cyber operations as a business. Often structured like a real company — devs build exploits, ops run them, others sell stolen data, and there's even customer support for ransomware payments.

- **Resources:** extensive. Profits from one campaign fund the next.
- **Sophistication:** high. May not develop true zero-days like nation states, but their tooling is mature.
- **Motivations:** money. Always money. Ransomware, banking trojans, payment-card theft, fraud-as-a-service.
- **Typical targets:** any org with payable assets or extortable data.

**Exam trap:** organized crime ≈ nation state on resources and sophistication. The tie-breaker is **motivation**: financial gain → organized crime; geopolitical → nation state. A ransomware crew with a "support line" is organized crime even though they look APT-grade.

### Shadow IT
Not an attacker in the traditional sense — a *group inside the organization* doing IT work outside the official IT department. They stand up their own cloud accounts, install their own apps, and connect their own infrastructure, usually with a department credit card and zero IT involvement.

- **Resources:** limited to their departmental budget, but a credit card and a cloud provider go a long way.
- **Sophistication:** generally low, especially on security. Few have IT backgrounds.
- **Motivations:** **not malice — convenience.** They want to move fast and IT slows them down with change control, budgeting, and reviews.
- **Risk profile:** no patching, no backup, no access control, no visibility into the data. A single misconfigured S3 bucket is the classic outcome.

**Exam trap:** Shadow IT is on the threat-actor list even though intent is benign. The exam tests whether you recognize that **lack of malice doesn't make it less risky** — unmanaged infrastructure with sensitive data is a real security problem regardless of intent.

## How to read scenario questions

For any actor the exam describes, work the four axes in order:

1. **Internal or external?** Insider and Shadow IT are internal; everything else is external by default.
2. **What are the resources?** "Limited," "well-funded," "extensive" — these words are clues.
3. **What's the sophistication?** "Custom tools," "zero-day," "advanced" → nation state, organized crime, hacktivist. "Publicly available script," "didn't understand" → unskilled.
4. **What's the motivation?** Financial → organized crime (external) or insider (internal). Political/philosophical → hacktivist. Espionage/war → nation state. Convenience → Shadow IT.

When two answers seem to fit, the motivation almost always breaks the tie. Read the stem twice for *why*.
