# 5.3 — Third-party Risk

CompTIA description: *"Explain the processes associated with third-party risk assessment and management."*

Every vendor relationship is a hole punched in your security perimeter — payroll providers, ISPs, SaaS apps, raw-materials suppliers all touch your data or your systems. The exam tests two things: **how you evaluate and monitor a vendor** (assessment, audit, supply-chain analysis, due diligence, ongoing monitoring) and **how you contractually bind them** (the seven agreement types and where each fits).

---

## Third-party Risk Assessment

The set of activities that surface, measure, and track the risk a vendor brings.

### Penetration testing of vendors
Active exploit attempts against the vendor's systems (or jointly contracted via a third pen-test specialist). Often required *by contract* on a recurring interval so both parties know how strong the security actually is.

**Rules of Engagement (ROE)** — the document scoping a pen test. Defines:

| ROE element | What it specifies |
|---|---|
| **Test type** | On-site physical, internal network, external (internet-side) |
| **Schedule** | Date/time windows; working hours vs after-hours |
| **IP ranges** | What's in scope |
| **Out-of-scope systems** | What must not be touched (e.g., production payment processor) |
| **Emergency contacts** | Who to call if something breaks |
| **Sensitive-data handling** | What the tester does with anything they find |

### Right-to-audit clause
Contract language reserving your authority to inspect the vendor's security controls on a recurring basis. Lets you (or a contracted third party) review access management, offboarding, password handling, VPN controls, and more. Often required by compliance regimes for high-trust vendors.

### Evidence of internal audits
Vendor produces their own audit results (or SOC 2, ISO 27001 certifications) for your review. Cheaper than your own audit; not as deep. Often paired with right-to-audit for spot-checks.

### Supply chain analysis
End-to-end review of how a product or service moves from raw materials → vendor → you → customer, and where security exposures live in that chain. The 2020 **SolarWinds** incident is the canonical example: an attacker compromised SolarWinds' build process, the malware was signed with a valid SolarWinds certificate, and ~18,000 customers received it as a routine update. After SolarWinds, supply-chain analysis stopped being optional for big networks.

### Independent assessments
Bring in an outsider whose perspective isn't shaped by your org's biases. Often surfaces blind spots an internal team can't see — a knowledgeable third party brings cross-industry pattern recognition.

### Due diligence
The investigation you do *before* signing — verifying claims about revenue, customer count, key personnel, certifications. May include background checks and reference interviews.

### Conflict of interest
A relationship that could compromise the vendor's or your judgment:

- Vendor also serves your largest competitor
- Vendor employs a relative of one of your executives
- Vendor offers gifts contingent on contract signing

Either party may need to walk away when one of these surfaces.

### Vendor monitoring (ongoing)
After signing, monitoring continues:

| Monitoring activity | What it watches |
|---|---|
| **Financial health checks** | Vendor going under = supply disruption + data risk |
| **IT security reviews** | Posture drift over time |
| **News / social media** | Breach reports, lawsuits, leadership changes |
| **Vendor questionnaire** | Periodic structured survey: due-diligence practices, DR plans, data-storage methods, security controls |

The questionnaire is the most-tested artifact — it's the structured ongoing mechanism that feeds back into the vendor risk register.

## Agreement Types

The contractual scaffolding that binds the vendor relationship. The exam tests *which agreement covers what* — match terms to artifacts.

| Agreement | Formality | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| **MOU** — Memorandum of Understanding | Informal, often non-binding | Broad goals of working together; often includes confidentiality language. Not a contract. |
| **MOA** — Memorandum of Agreement | More formal than MOU; may have binding language | Describes the relationship in more detail than MOU; still typically not legally enforceable as a contract |
| **MSA** — Master Service Agreement | Legal contract; framework | Sets baseline terms (billing, payment, liability) for an ongoing relationship. Future-specific work is added under it. |
| **SOW / WO** — Statement of Work / Work Order | Legal; specific scope | Specific job under an MSA: scope, location, deliverables, schedule. Avoids re-negotiating the basics every project. |
| **SLA** — Service Level Agreement | Legal; measurable | Minimum service requirements: uptime, max downtime, response time, on-site spares |
| **NDA** — Non-Disclosure Agreement | Legal contract | Confidentiality. Unilateral (one-way), bilateral / mutual (both ways), or multilateral (3+ parties) |
| **BPA** — Business Partners Agreement | Legal contract | Financial details of a partnership: ownership stakes, decision-making authority, dispute and dissolution handling |

### Key distinctions

**MOU vs MOA vs MSA** — the formality ladder. MOU is "we agree in principle." MOA is "we agree with detail." MSA is "we have a binding contractual framework." The exam may show all three as options and want you to pick by level of formality.

**MSA vs SOW** — MSA is the *framework* (one document); SOW is *one job* under it (many documents over time). MSA holds payment terms and liability; SOW holds the scope and deliverables for a specific engagement.

**SLA vs SOW** — Both describe what the vendor delivers, but SLA is *ongoing minimum service* (uptime, response time) while SOW is *a specific scoped job* (build this thing by this date).

**NDA flavors:**
- **Unilateral** — only one party must keep confidentiality (you tell them; they keep quiet)
- **Bilateral / Mutual** — both parties keep each other's information confidential
- **Multilateral** — three or more parties

**Trap:** A *contract* requires legal enforceability. MOU and MOA are typically *not* contracts. MSA, SOW, SLA, NDA, BPA are. If the exam asks "which is a legally binding contract," eliminate MOU and MOA first.

## How to read scenario questions

For a 5.3 scenario, work in this order:

1. **Is this a pre-sign or post-sign question?** Pre-sign → due diligence, conflict of interest, MOU/MOA exploration. Post-sign → audits, questionnaires, ongoing monitoring, SLA enforcement.
2. **For an agreement question,** ask: does it set *intent* (MOU), *detailed terms* (MOA), a *framework for ongoing work* (MSA), a *specific job* (SOW), *service minimums* (SLA), *confidentiality* (NDA), or *partnership economics* (BPA)?
3. **For supply-chain questions,** check if the issue arose from the vendor's vendor — that's a supply-chain problem, not a direct-vendor problem.
4. **For a pen test question,** identify what the ROE governs (scope, schedule, IPs, sensitive-data handling).
