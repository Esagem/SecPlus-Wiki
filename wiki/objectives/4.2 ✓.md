# 4.2 — Asset Management

CompTIA description: *"Explain the security implications of proper hardware, software, and data asset management."*

Every asset has a security lifecycle that begins at acquisition and ends at disposal. The objective is the full sweep: procurement → assignment → tracking → enumeration → sanitization or destruction → retention. The exam tests whether you know the right step for the right phase.

---

## The lifecycle

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Acquisition / Procurement** | Need identified → budget approved → vendor negotiated → invoice received → goods delivered |
| **Assignment & ownership** | Asset entered in tracking system, owner assigned, tagged |
| **Tracking** | Ongoing inventory, help-desk integration, enumeration of components |
| **Sanitization / Disposal** | Reuse → secure wipe; dispose → physical destruction or certified third-party destruction |
| **Data retention** | Policies and regulatory requirements for keeping (and eventually deleting) data |

---

## Procurement and acquisition

Acquisition is rarely a single step. End user identifies a need → IT and Purchasing evaluate → budget check → formal approvals → negotiation with the supplier → invoice with payment terms (net 30, net 60, payable on receipt).

Security implications are easy to miss here: any contract sets the terms for software licensing, data handling, and (often) liability for breaches. The negotiation phase is where security requirements get baked into the agreement — once the invoice arrives, it's too late.

---

## Tracking, ownership, and enumeration

### Assigning ownership

Every asset has a **named owner** in the tracking system. When something needs to change on a device, you visit the owner — there's no ambiguity about who has the laptop.

### Hardware vs software

Tracking distinguishes between hardware and software because tax treatment differs:

| Type | Capital vs operating | Tax treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Hardware** | Capital expenditure (CapEx) | Depreciates over time |
| **Software** | Operating expense (OpEx) | Expensed; doesn't depreciate |

### Enumeration

A "desktop computer" is one asset, but inside it are CPU, memory, storage drives, keyboard, mouse, peripherals. The tracking system can present it as a single record *or* break it down by component — useful for help-desk tickets (technician knows the exact model) and for security (a missing drive is detectable).

### Asset tags

Physical labels — barcode, RFID, or numeric — tying the device back to its tracking record. Doubles as a security feature: if the device is lost or stolen, the tag identifies the owner and may deter resale.

## Sanitization and disposal

The choice depends on what you intend to do with the device afterward.

| Goal | Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Reuse internally**, same drive | **Secure delete** (cryptographic wipe / overwrite) | Data unrecoverable; drive remains usable |
| **Reuse internally**, sensitive files only | Delete the specific sensitive files | Less drastic; for low-risk reassignments |
| **Permanent destruction, in-house** | Drill, hammer, shredder, pulverizer | Drive becomes physically unusable |
| **Permanent destruction, hard drives only** | **Degaussing** (strong electromagnet) | Erases data AND destroys the drive |
| **Permanent destruction, total** | **Incineration** | Absolute end state |
| **Bulk destruction** | **Third-party destruction service** | Provides a **Certificate of Destruction** documenting each drive |

> **Exam trap:** Degaussing works on magnetic hard drives. It does *not* reliably destroy data on SSDs (no moving magnetic media). For SSDs, use cryptographic erase or physical destruction.

> **Exam trap:** A **Certificate of Destruction** is the documented evidence that a third party destroyed media. The exam loves this term — when the question is "you outsourced destruction, what do you get back?", the answer is the certificate.

### Sanitize vs destroy — the distinction

- **Sanitize** = data is unrecoverable, drive may still work. Cryptographic wipe, secure overwrite.
- **Destroy** = drive is physically rendered unusable. Shred, drill, degauss, incinerate.

Reuse → sanitize. Dispose → destroy.

## Data retention

Retention is the *opposite* problem from destruction: keeping data long enough to satisfy a regulation, lawsuit, or business need.

### Why retain

- **Regulatory mandate** — emails for a defined number of years (SOX, HIPAA, etc.); financial records for set durations.
- **Backup / disaster recovery** — accidental deletes, ransomware recovery, site failover.
- **Internal investigation** — long-running incidents discovered months or years later.

### What gets retained

Retention applies to the original data *and* every copy — primary storage, backups, replicas, offsite archives. A retention policy that only addresses the primary copy misses the secondary copies that are equally subject to subpoena.

### Different data, different rules

Different categories of data may have very different retention periods. Email might be one duration; financial records another; security logs another; PII another. The retention policy enumerates the categories and their requirements.

> **Exam trap:** Retention and destruction are paired — a retention policy says "keep email for X years," but it also implies "after X years, delete it." Holding data longer than required creates legal exposure (it can be subpoenaed) and storage cost. The correct lifecycle is keep for the required period, then sanitize/destroy on a schedule.

---

## How to read scenario questions

1. **Where in the lifecycle?** Acquisition, tracking, reuse, disposal, retention.
2. **Reuse or dispose?** Reuse → secure delete (sanitize). Dispose → physical destruction.
3. **Hard drive or SSD?** Degaussing works on hard drives, not SSDs.
4. **In-house or third-party destruction?** Third-party means a Certificate of Destruction comes back.
5. **Retention question?** Look for the regulatory or policy driver — retention isn't arbitrary; it's mandated.
