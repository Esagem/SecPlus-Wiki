# 5.6 — Security Awareness and Training

CompTIA description: *"Given a scenario, implement security awareness practices."*

Users are the largest, most-attacked, most-bypassable layer of any defense. This objective splits cleanly: **security awareness** is the ongoing program (phishing campaigns, anomaly recognition, metrics, awareness team), and **user training** is the structured education users receive — ideally before they touch the network. Both reinforce the *directive* control type from 1.1: telling people what to do works only if it's paired with measurement.

---

## Security Awareness

The continuous loop of *test → train → measure → repeat* that keeps users sensitized to threats over time.

### Phishing campaigns
Run your own phishing emails against your own users to see what they do. Internal-built or third-party platforms; both report opens, clicks, attachment runs, and credential submissions back to a central console. Users who click are auto-enrolled in additional training. This is also a great test of your *email filter* — ideally those test emails get caught before they ever land.

### What users should recognize in a phishing email

| Signal | What to look for |
|---|---|
| **Spelling / grammar errors** | In the body or — increasingly important — in the link domain itself |
| **Domain inconsistency** | Sender display name doesn't match domain; reply-to differs from from; lookalike domain |
| **Unusual attachments** | Unexpected office docs, ZIPs, ISOs, or anything from someone who normally doesn't send files |
| **Personal info or credential requests** | Legitimate orgs don't ask you to "re-verify" passwords or SSNs via email link |
| **Email construction inconsistencies** | Mismatched logos, awkward salutations, urgency-laden subject lines |

**Reporting:** users should have a well-known, frictionless way to report suspected phishing to the IT security team — a button in their email client, a forward address, or a help-desk shortcut.

### Anomalous behavior recognition
Beyond phishing, the program watches user activity for three patterns of anomaly:

| Pattern | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Risky behavior** | Modifying the hosts file, replacing core OS files, uploading sensitive files to external sites |
| **Unexpected behavior** | Login from another country, sudden large data transfers, off-hours activity that breaks pattern |
| **Unintentional behavior** | Typing the wrong domain, misplacing a USB drive, misconfigured security settings — human error |

**Trap:** Unintentional anomalies are still anomalies worth catching. The exam may classify a typo or a misconfiguration as "anomalous behavior" — that's intended.

### Monitoring and metrics
A user-aware program is only as good as what it measures. Common metrics:

- **Phishing click rates** over time and by department
- **Password manager adoption**
- **MFA enrollment / use**
- **Time-to-report** for suspected phishing
- **Repeat-clicker rate** (the same users falling repeatedly)

Repeat-clickers point to where extra training, mentoring, or stricter local configurations are needed.

### The security awareness team
A specialized team focused on user-side issues — not the SOC. Responsibilities:

| Function | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Create training materials** | Email, posters, online modules, in-person training |
| **Role-based customization** | Different training for accounting, dev, executives, third parties |
| **Compliance-driven content** | HIPAA training for healthcare; PCI training for cardholder data |
| **Metric reporting** | Track effectiveness over time; report to stakeholders |

## User Training

The structured education users receive. The exam tests *what topics* training covers and *when* it should be delivered.

### Timing
Ideally **before users connect to the network for the first time.** Onboarding training is more effective and less costly than remediation after an incident.

### Role-based training
One size doesn't fit. Different roles need different focus:

| Role | Training emphasis |
|---|---|
| **Accounting** | Wire-fraud / BEC scams, financial-data handling, SOX |
| **Shipping / receiving** | Physical-access scams, package-tampering awareness |
| **Healthcare** | HIPAA, PHI handling |
| **Developers** | Secure coding, SDLC controls |
| **Executives** | Spear-phishing / whaling awareness, mobile device hardening |
| **Contractors / partners / suppliers** | Specific terms of their access; NDAs |

Track who has been trained and who hasn't — it's part of compliance evidence.

### Documented and accessible policies
Every user must be able to reach the security policies — via intranet and the employee handbook. A policy nobody can find isn't a policy that works.

### Situational awareness
Train users to look for threats *while doing whatever they're doing.* Key categories:

| Threat | What to flag |
|---|---|
| **Phishing emails** | Suspicious links, attachments, requests for credentials |
| **Smishing (SMS phishing)** | Text messages with urgent action requests |
| **Unusual URLs** | Lookalikes, IP-only URLs, IDN-homoglyph domains |
| **Physical attacks** | Suspicious envelopes, unknown USB drives, tailgating |

**The USB-drive scenario:** an envelope arrives looking official, with a USB drive inside. Plugging it in could install malware (USB-rubber-ducky style) or trigger autorun. Trained users hand it to IT security.

### Insider threats
Hardest threat category to detect because the actor is authorized. Multi-factored approach:

| Defense | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Multiple approvals** | Critical changes require multiple authorized parties (separation of duties / two-person control) |
| **Active file monitoring** | Alert on changes to critical files, with hard-to-bypass logging |
| **Difficulty of subversion** | Make the bypass paths expensive — auditable, multi-step, observable |

### Password management
Training plus enforcement. Standards (objective 5.1) set the length and complexity; training tells users *why* and *how* — including the safe use of password managers.

### Removable media and cables
Users plug in things they shouldn't:

- Unknown USB drives → malware injection or HID-attack devices
- Borrowed charging cables → "juice-jacking" via cables that exfiltrate data while charging

Train users to use only known media and to avoid public USB charging (use the wall outlet with a personal cable or a USB data-blocker).

### Social engineering recognition
Train on common patterns — pretexting, urgency, authority impersonation, baiting — so users can identify and *report* attempts to the security team rather than respond to them.

### Operational security (OpSec) mindset
Encourage users to view their own work through an attacker's lens. What sensitive data am I exposing in this Zoom screenshare? What does my out-of-office message reveal? What's on the conference-call agenda visible behind me?

### Remote / work-from-home considerations
Workers off-site introduce risks:

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Family / friends accessing work device** | Standalone work device; no shared accounts; session locking |
| **Weak home network** | VPN; endpoint security on the device itself |
| **Mixed home/work devices** | Endpoint controls; restricted data classes |

VPN access from outside the office often needs *additional* security, not less — the device is outside your perimeter.

## How to read scenario questions

For a 5.6 scenario, work in this order:

1. **Is the question about a TEST or a TRAINING activity?** Internal phishing emails sent to users = phishing campaign (test). Onboarding session = training.
2. **For phishing-recognition questions,** match the signal: grammar / domain / attachments / credential requests / unusual construction.
3. **For anomaly questions,** classify the behavior: risky (e.g., file-replacement), unexpected (e.g., overseas login), unintentional (e.g., typo).
4. **For insider-threat questions,** prefer answers that combine *multiple* controls: separation of duties + monitoring + auditable change paths.
5. **For physical / removable-media questions,** the right answer is almost always "don't plug it in / report to security."
