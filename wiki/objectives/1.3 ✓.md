# 1.3 — Change Management

CompTIA description: *"Explain the importance of change management processes and the impact to security."*

Change management exists because in any environment past a handful of devices, ad-hoc changes break things — and broken things create security gaps. This objective has two halves: the **governance** side (who approves what, who owns it, who gets notified) and the **technical** side (how the change is actually executed and rolled back). The exam tests vocabulary from both.

---

## The two halves

| Half | Lives at | Owns | Decides |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Business / governance** | Change Control Board (CCB) | The risk decision | *Whether* the change happens, *when*, and *who's affected* |
| **Technical** | The technicians | The execution | *How* the change is implemented, tested, and reverted |

The CCB never executes; the technicians never unilaterally approve. The split exists to prevent both "shadow IT" (changes without review) and "approval by the person doing the work" (no separation of duties).

---

## The change control process

A typical flow, in order:

1. **Request** — the owner of the application or data fills out a formal change request form.
2. **Scope** — what systems, users, and data are touched.
3. **Schedule** — date, time, and duration of the change window.
4. **Impact analysis** — who and what will be affected; expected downtime.
5. **Risk analysis** — what could go wrong, including the risk of *not* changing (unpatched vulnerability, expired support).
6. **CCB approval / denial** — based on risk vs. benefit, often timed against business cycles.
7. **Implementation** — technicians execute inside the approved window.
8. **User verification** — owner confirms the system works post-change.
9. **Documentation update** — diagrams, configs, IP records, runbooks updated.

### Owners vs. stakeholders

| Role | Who | Does what |
|---|---|---|
| **Owner** | The person/department that owns the affected app or data | Initiates the request, tests after change, doesn't typically execute |
| **Stakeholders** | Everyone meaningfully affected by the change | Provides input, must be identified by the IT team (not always obvious) |

**Trap:** Stakeholders are *broader than you think*. A "shipping label software update" might also affect accounting (reporting), customer experience (delivery), revenue recognition, and ultimately the CEO. Failing to identify the full stakeholder set is a classic change-management gap.

---

## Risk analysis — both directions

Every change has bidirectional risk:

| If you DO change | If you DON'T change |
|---|---|
| The patch may break a working system | The known vulnerability stays open |
| Update could corrupt data | App may stop working when a dependency upgrades |
| Service may not start cleanly | Compliance baseline drifts |

The CCB weighs both. "We can't apply this patch this week" is a *risk acceptance decision*, not the absence of one.

---

## Testing and backout

### Sandbox testing environment

A duplicate of production used to validate changes without user impact. Lets you test the upgrade *and* the contingency plan before touching real systems. "It worked in the lab" is the bar.

### Backout plan

The documented procedure to revert if something goes wrong. Some changes are trivial to undo (uninstall a patch); others are not (schema migrations, firmware updates). Either way, the plan must exist before the change runs.

> Belt-and-suspenders: always take a **full backup** of the affected system before the change, even if you have a backout plan. If the backout itself fails, the backup is the floor.

### Maintenance windows

Pre-defined times when changes are allowed — typically off-hours, weekends, or holidays. Some businesses (retail during the holiday season, healthcare during peak care periods) impose **change freezes** where no changes are permitted except emergency fixes.

**Trap:** A 24/7 operation may have no "off hours" at all. Those environments use **primary/secondary failover**: update the secondary, switch traffic to it, then update the former primary. Lets you make changes without scheduled downtime.

## Technical execution

### Allow lists and deny lists

| List | Default behavior | What you specify |
|---|---|---|
| **Allow list** | Block everything | Apps/IPs that ARE permitted (whitelist) |
| **Deny list** | Permit everything | Apps/IPs that are NOT permitted (blacklist) |

Anti-malware is effectively a giant deny list of known-bad code. A locked-down kiosk is an allow list. Allow lists are more secure but more restrictive; deny lists are more flexible but assume you know all the bad things.

### Scope vs. necessary expansion

A change is approved for a specific scope — you don't get free rein to make other changes just because the window is open. But the *primary* change sometimes requires secondary modifications (e.g., updating a config file in order to install the driver). Well-run organizations document policy for handling these in-scope-but-not-explicitly-listed sub-changes.

### Downtime, reboots, restarts

Three different levels of disruption:

| Action | Disruption | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Application restart** | Just that app | New executable, config reloaded by the app itself |
| **Service restart** | One service / daemon | Background service update (Windows Services, `systemctl restart`) |
| **Full reboot** | Whole OS | Kernel update, driver change, registry change that loads at boot |

A reboot also verifies the system *can* recover from a power cycle — useful side effect.

## Legacy applications

Older apps still running production, often:

- No longer supported by the vendor (or vendor defunct).
- "Don't touch this" institutional knowledge.
- Idiosyncratic dependencies — specific OS versions, specific libraries.

Strategy: **document the install, configuration, and dependencies** so the legacy app enters the normal support cycle. The first person to document it becomes the de facto expert. The alternative — leaving it as forbidden knowledge — guarantees an eventual outage no one can fix.

---

## Dependencies

A change to one component often forces changes to others. Examples:

- Firewall management software requires firewalls running new firmware → update firewalls first, then management software.
- A library upgrade breaks an old app → must upgrade or replace the app first.
- A service won't start until a sibling service is also updated.

Dependencies turn a one-component change into a multi-component change — and they're easy to miss during planning. Good change requests enumerate them; the CCB checks for them.

---

## Documentation and version control

Every change must update the documentation that describes the current state — network diagrams, IP plans, config inventories, runbooks. Without this, the wiki of how things work drifts immediately out of sync with reality.

**Version control** keeps the artifacts themselves under change tracking — router configs, OS images, application releases, infrastructure-as-code. Lets you see exactly what changed between version *A* and version *B* and revert if needed. Some platforms have version control built in (e.g., network OSes with config rollback); for others, you add a third-party system.

---

## How to read scenario questions

For 1.3 questions, the prompt usually gives you a scenario and asks "what step was missed" or "what role does X play."

- "Made the change directly in production without testing" → missing **sandbox testing**.
- "No way to revert when the change broke" → missing **backout plan**.
- "Update broke accounting reports nobody warned about" → missing **stakeholder identification**.
- "Tech also patched two unrelated systems during the window" → out-of-scope; violates the approved scope.
- "Vendor app, no docs, only Bob understands it" → **legacy app**; document it.
- "Need to update firewall mgmt; firewalls are on older firmware" → **dependency**.
