# 1.1 — Security Controls

CompTIA description: *"Compare and contrast various types of security controls."*

The whole objective is classifying controls along two orthogonal axes — the **category** (how it's implemented) and the **type** (what it does in the event lifecycle). Master the 4×6 matrix and you've got it.

---

## The framework

Every real-world control is both *a type* and *a category*. The axes are independent.

| Axis | Description | Values |
|---|---|---|
| **Category** | How the control is implemented | Technical · Managerial · Operational · Physical |
| **Type** | What the control does in the event lifecycle | Preventive · Deterrent · Detective · Corrective · Compensating · Directive |

A door lock = preventive (type) + physical (category). A firewall rule = preventive + technical. An onboarding policy = preventive + managerial. The exam loves to ask you to classify a scenario along both axes.

---

## The 4×6 matrix

|  | Technical | Managerial | Operational | Physical |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Preventive** | Firewall rule | Onboarding policy | Guard checking ID | Door lock |
| **Deterrent** | Login splash screen | Threat of demotion | Reception desk | Warning sign |
| **Detective** | System logs | Periodic log-review policy | Property patrol | Motion detector |
| **Corrective** | Restore from backup | IR reporting policy | Calling law enforcement | Fire extinguisher |
| **Compensating** | Block traffic vs. patching | Separation of duties | Multiple guards | Backup generator |
| **Directive** | "Store sensitive files in encrypted folder" | Compliance policy | Security training | "Authorized personnel only" sign |

Each cell is *an example*, not the definition. Plenty of valid alternative examples for any cell.

---

## The 4 categories — the "how"

### Technical
Implemented by systems, software, or hardware acting without human intervention. Firewall, IDS/IPS, antivirus, OS policies, ACLs, encryption.

### Managerial
Implemented by policy and procedure. Onboarding rules, compliance policies, change-management processes. Often documented in a security policy.

### Operational
Implemented by people performing tasks. Guards, trainers, incident responders, awareness programs. If a human does the work day-to-day, it's operational.

### Physical
Implemented by tangible barriers or devices. Locks, fences, badge readers, fire extinguishers, generators.

> **Operational ≠ Technical.** A camera recording to a server is technical. A guard watching the camera feed is operational. The same incident can involve both.

---

## The 6 types — the "what"

### Preventive — blocks
Blocks access before an event can occur. The first line of defense — make the bad thing impossible.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | Firewall rule denying inbound port 3389 from the internet |
| Managerial | Onboarding policy requiring background checks before granting access |
| Operational | Guard at a shack checking IDs before letting people drive in |
| Physical | Door lock, mantrap, fence |

**Preventive vs Deterrent:** Preventive *blocks*. If the control is working, the event cannot happen. Deterrent only *discourages* — the event is still physically possible. A locked door is preventive; a "no trespassing" sign is deterrent. Both are common on the same door — defense in depth.

**Trap:** An IDS is *not* preventive — it watches and reports. An IPS *is* preventive — it blocks. The "P" matters.

### Deterrent — discourages
Discourages an actor without physically preventing the act. The attack remains possible; the calculus changes.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | Login banner / splash screen warning about unauthorized access and monitoring |
| Managerial | Stated policy of demotion or dismissal for unauthorized access |
| Operational | Front reception desk visibly greeting / logging visitors |
| Physical | "Beware of dog," CCTV-in-use, "trespassers will be prosecuted" signs |

**Why deterrents matter despite not blocking:** Most casual attackers do a cost-benefit. A deterrent raises the perceived cost (legal, social, embarrassment) enough to redirect them. Useless against a determined attacker; effective against opportunists.

**Trap:** "Discourages," "may think twice," "warns of consequences" → deterrent. A login banner that just warns is deterrent. The login *screen* that requires a password is preventive. Same screen, different controls layered.

### Detective — identifies
Identifies and reports that an event has occurred (or is occurring). Doesn't prevent and doesn't fix — tells you something happened.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | System logs, IDS alerts, SIEM correlation rules |
| Managerial | Periodic policy of reviewing login reports |
| Operational | Security guard patrolling the property |
| Physical | Motion detector, glass-break sensor, CCTV (when used for monitoring) |

**Detective vs Preventive:** Preventive *blocks*. Detective only *notices*. IDS = detective. IPS = preventive. Same data, different action.

**Why detective controls earn their keep:** You can't fully prevent everything. Detective controls catch what slips through — they enable triage, response, and lessons-learned. A breach you never detect is the worst kind.

**Trap:** CCTV is layered — as a *visible sign* it's deterrent; as a *recorded system reviewed for incidents* it's detective. Reviewing logs is *managerial detective*. The logs themselves are *technical detective*. Same activity, different control depending on what's being asked.

### Corrective — undoes
Applied after an event has been detected, to reverse or limit its impact and restore operations.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | Restore from backup after a ransomware infection |
| Managerial | Policy requiring all security incidents be reported and rolled into IR |
| Operational | Calling law enforcement after a break-in |
| Physical | Fire extinguisher stopping a fire from spreading |

**The lifecycle position:** Deterrent → Preventive → Detective → Corrective. Compensating and Directive sit sideways.

**Corrective vs Compensating:** Corrective acts *after* the event already happened — past tense, undo or limit. Compensating *substitutes for* a control you can't deploy — present continuous, fills a gap. The fire extinguisher *after* a fire = corrective. The same extinguisher stationed *because sprinklers aren't installed yet* = compensating.

**Trap:** "Restores," "recovers," "reverses" → corrective. Backups are corrective; the *policy that requires backups* is managerial — depends on what the question highlights.

### Compensating — substitutes
Substitutes for a control you can't or haven't yet deployed. Usually temporary; bridges the gap until the real fix lands.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | Firewall rule blocking the exploit path while waiting on a vendor patch |
| Managerial | Separation of duties when no single person should hold the full action |
| Operational | Two security guards on overlapping shifts so no one has sole access |
| Physical | Backup generator running while main power is restored |

**Why compensating is its own type:** It's a *role* a control plays, not a flavor of mechanism. The firewall rule blocking the exploit is preventive-technical in mechanism — but in this context it's *compensating* because it stands in for the patch that should have been there. The same firewall rule deployed permanently as the intended defense is just preventive.

**Trap:** Look for "until," "while waiting," "temporary," "instead of" — strong signals for compensating. In PCI-DSS context, "compensating controls" are formally defined and require documentation showing they meet the original control's intent.

### Directive — instructs
Instructs people to behave securely. Relies on cooperation — the weakest type because there's no enforcement.

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | "Store sensitive files in the encrypted folder" — leaves classification to the user |
| Managerial | Compliance policy documenting required procedures |
| Operational | Security awareness training |
| Physical | "Authorized personnel only" sign (no lock) |

**Why it's the weakest type:** Every other type either acts on the threat (preventive/detective/corrective/compensating) or threatens the actor (deterrent). Directive *asks*. If the person ignores the instruction, the control fails silently — no friction, no log, no alarm.

That's why directive controls almost always need to be paired with something else. A "do not enter" sign + a locked door. A compliance policy + an audit. Training + monitoring.

**Trap:** "Instructs," "directs," "requires the user to" → directive. Easy to confuse with *managerial*: managerial is the *category* (set by policy/people). Directive is the *type* (relies on cooperation). A compliance policy can be both — managerial category, directive type.

---

## How to read scenario questions

For any control the exam describes, ask in this order:

1. **What does it do?** → that's the *type* (preventive, deterrent, detective, corrective, compensating, directive).
2. **How is it implemented?** → that's the *category* (technical, managerial, operational, physical).
3. **If it's compensating, what is it compensating for?** Compensating only makes sense as a substitute for something you can't do directly (yet).
