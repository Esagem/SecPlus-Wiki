# 4.8 — Incident Response

CompTIA description: *"Explain appropriate incident response activities."*

Three threads run through this objective: the **NIST IR lifecycle** (the four-phase model from SP 800-61r2), the **planning exercises** that prepare you to run that lifecycle (tabletop, simulation, root-cause analysis, threat hunting), and **digital forensics** (RFC 3227, chain of custody, legal hold, e-discovery). The exam tests both the phase names and the procedural details — especially what must happen *before* and *after* the actual incident.

---

## The NIST IR lifecycle

**NIST Special Publication 800-61 Revision 2** — *Computer Security Incident Handling Guide* — is the canonical reference. Four phases:

| Phase | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Preparation** | Build the team, tools, contacts, and playbooks before anything happens |
| **Detection & Analysis** | Recognize an incident has occurred; characterize scope and impact |
| **Containment, Eradication, & Recovery** | Stop the spread; remove the cause; restore operations |
| **Post-incident Activity** | Lessons learned; update playbooks and controls |

> **Exam trap:** Memorize the four phases — and notice that *containment, eradication, and recovery* are one phase, not three. CompTIA loves to combine or separate them in answer choices.

### Preparation — what to have ready

- **Communications list** — up-to-date contacts for IR team, leadership, legal, PR, external authorities. Out-of-band (printed) copy in case email is compromised.
- **Incident go-bag** — laptops with forensic software, write-blockers, removable media for imaging, digital camera/video.
- **Reference material** — server documentation, network diagrams, security baselines, **file hashes of critical files**.
- **Mitigation kit** — known-good OS images, application installers, recovery tools.
- **Policies and procedures** — written playbooks so the team isn't improvising during a live incident.

### Detection & Analysis — what triggers it

A real attack mixed with constant background noise is hard to distinguish. Signals to weigh:

- **IPS/IDS alerts** (buffer-overflow attempt, exploitation pattern).
- **Antivirus / EDR reports** (malware on an endpoint).
- **Configuration changes** in security settings (an attacker disabling logging).
- **Unusual traffic volume** — a large outbound spike may be data exfiltration.
- **Log review** — web server logs showing vulnerability scans, auth logs showing failed bursts.
- **Attacker contact** — uncommon, but ransomware/extortion crews sometimes notify the victim directly.

### Containment / Eradication / Recovery

Stop the spread first; investigate further only when safe. A **sandbox** lets you run suspected malware in isolation to study behavior — though sophisticated malware detects sandboxes and changes its behavior (or deletes itself) when it does.

**Recovery actions:**
- Remove malware or **reimage** affected systems.
- **Disable** any breached or attacker-created user accounts.
- **Patch the vulnerability** the attacker used.
- **Restore from known-good backups** or **reinstall from original media**.
- Lock things down so the attacker can't return through the same path.

### Post-incident — lessons learned

The **post-incident meeting** is held as soon as possible after recovery (memory degrades fast). Questions to answer:

- What exactly happened, and when?
- How well did the documented process work?
- What indicators did we miss?
- What changes go into the playbook for next time?

## Incident planning — exercises

You can't train for an incident *during* an incident. Three preparation methods:

### Tabletop exercises

Everyone sits at a table and *talks through* a scenario. A few hours, one specific scenario, no production systems touched. Cheap and fast; tests the **process**, not the technical skills.

### Simulations

Live but contained tests:

| Simulation | What it tests |
|---|---|
| **Phishing simulation** | Email filters; user click rate; help-desk and SOC response |
| **Help-desk pretext call** | Whether the help desk can be socially engineered into a password reset |
| **Controlled data exfiltration** | Whether DLP and monitoring catch the outbound traffic |

Phishing sims by a third party are common — the report shows who clicked, whether email filters caught it, whether anti-phishing redirects worked, and which users need follow-up training.

### Root-cause analysis (RCA)

A real incident usually involves *multiple* root causes — a missed patch *and* a weak password *and* a user clicking a link. The RCA reconstructs the sequence from logs and artifacts, identifies every contributing failure, and feeds them into preparation for next time.

> **Exam trap:** Don't stop at the first root cause. Real incidents are layered — a phishing email is the entry point, but missing MFA, a weak password policy, and an unpatched server are all root causes too.

### Threat hunting

**Proactive** identification of vulnerabilities and attacker activity *before* an incident. Threat-hunting actions include monitoring new CVE announcements, verifying that patches are deployed, updating firewall rules in response to fresh threat intel, and tuning detection signatures. Often paired with automation so detections fire even when no human is actively watching.

## Digital forensics

The collection, preservation, analysis, and reporting of digital evidence — used internally to understand an incident, and externally as evidence in legal proceedings that may occur years later. **RFC 3227** — *Guidelines for Evidence Collection and Archiving* — is the reference for best practices.

### Legal hold

A formal request, usually from a lawyer or other legal entity, sent to a **data custodian**, requiring specific data to be preserved.

- The custodian identifies the data described in the hold.
- Data is acquired and stored in a dedicated repository for **ESI** — **Electronically Stored Information**.
- Acquisition may require format conversion (e.g., proprietary mail-client storage → plain email text).
- Once in the ESI repository, the data must be preserved in its pristine form until released.

### Chain of custody

Documents who touched the evidence, when, and what they did with it. Required to demonstrate the evidence has not been tampered with.

| Domain | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Sealed evidence bag; signature on the seal each time it's opened |
| **Digital** | Cryptographic **hashes** and **digital signatures** computed at acquisition; recomputed at each handoff |

> **Exam trap:** Chain of custody isn't just about the data itself — it's the *paper trail* (or its digital equivalent) showing who had access. Hashes alone aren't chain of custody; the hash plus the documented log of access is.

### Acquisition

Evidence can come from many places. The exam wants you to recognize the breadth:

- **Disk images** (full bit-for-bit copies)
- **Memory** (RAM contents, especially for malware analysis)
- **Firmware**
- **Files** — including hidden, temporary, and deleted
- **Servers, network devices, firewalls, IPSes** — their logs and configs
- **VM snapshots** — full capture of state, disks, and memory
- **Logs**
- **Recycle bin / trash**
- **Browser bookmarks, saved logins, cache, history**

### Preservation — work on copies

Always work from **copies** of acquired evidence, never the original. The original goes into evidence storage; analysis happens on a verified duplicate. Hashes proved at acquisition let analysts confirm later that the copy matches the original.

### Live acquisition

Modern endpoints often have full-disk encryption. Powering off the system can lock the data away forever (no recovery key, no decryption). **Live acquisition** captures memory and disk contents while the system is still running and the encryption keys are still in RAM. Document the live state, then proceed.

### Reporting

Forensic reports typically follow a structured shape:

1. **Summary / overview** — what the event was and why this acquisition was done.
2. **Detailed acquisition steps** — every step, with integrity checks, in enough detail that a third party can verify it.
3. **Analysis** — factual description of the data's structure and what it shows.
4. **Conclusion** — analyst's interpretation of what the data indicates about the event.

### E-discovery

The process of **collecting, preparing, reviewing, interpreting, and producing** electronic documents — typically in response to legal action. **E-discovery is collection-focused; it does not require analysis.** A forensics team may receive the e-discovery output (e.g., a disk image) and then perform the actual analysis.

| | E-discovery | Forensics |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Collect and produce documents for legal use | Determine what happened and how |
| Includes analysis? | No | Yes |
| Output | The data itself | A report explaining the data |

> **Exam trap:** "Collecting and producing data" → e-discovery. "Analyzing the data to determine what happened" → forensics. The two often work together but answer different questions.

## How to read scenario questions

1. **Which phase of the lifecycle?** Preparation (before), Detection & Analysis (now), Containment/Eradication/Recovery (during), Post-incident (after).
2. **Exercise vs real incident?** Tabletop and simulation are pre-incident testing. RCA is post-incident reconstruction. Threat hunting is proactive between incidents.
3. **Legal involvement?** Legal hold → preserve. Chain of custody → document. E-discovery → produce. Forensics → analyze.
4. **Encryption present?** Live acquisition before shutting down.
5. **One root cause or multiple?** Real incidents are layered — phishing + missing MFA + weak password + unpatched system can all be root causes simultaneously.
