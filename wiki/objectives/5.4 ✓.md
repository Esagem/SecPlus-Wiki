# 5.4 — Compliance and Privacy

CompTIA description: *"Summarize elements of effective security compliance."*

Compliance is meeting external rules — laws, regulations, contractual obligations — and proving you meet them. Privacy is one specific compliance domain that's risen to first-class importance because so many laws now define a *data subject's* rights over information about themselves. The exam tests **which regulation governs what** (SOX, HIPAA, GLBA, GDPR), **what failure costs** (fines, reputational damage, license loss, contract breach), and **who plays which role** when personal data is involved.

---

## Compliance fundamentals

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Internal compliance** | Adherence to your own internal policies, usually led by a **Chief Compliance Officer (CCO)** |
| **External compliance** | Meeting requirements set by laws, regulators, or contractual third parties |
| **Reporting** | Periodic compliance reports — annual or on a defined cadence. Missing or falsifying = penalties |
| **Penalties** | Fines, loss of employment, **incarceration** in worst cases, loss of licenses, contract termination |

### Key regulations

| Regulation | Domain | What it requires |
|---|---|---|
| **SOX** — Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 | Financial reporting for public companies | Integrity and accountability of financial data and reporting |
| **HIPAA** — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act | US healthcare | Privacy and security of Protected Health Information (PHI) |
| **GLBA** — Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 | US financial institutions | Customer privacy disclosure and protection |
| **GDPR** — General Data Protection Regulation | EU residents | Privacy and control of personal data for EU data subjects |
| **PCI-DSS** | Payment card data (contractual, not law) | Annual assessments, control requirements for cardholder data |

**Trap:** PCI-DSS is *contractual*, not law. It's enforced by the card brands through merchant agreements. You can be out of PCI compliance without breaking a law — but you can lose the right to accept cards.

### HIPAA non-compliance penalties (illustrative scale)
The transcript walks through the HIPAA fine ladder as an example of how severe compliance can be:

| Violation | Fine ceiling | Possible prison |
|---|---|---|
| Standard violation | $50,000 | up to 1 year |
| False pretenses | $100,000 | up to 5 years |
| Intent to sell / commercial advantage / malicious harm | $250,000 | up to 10 years |
| Civil per-violation (identical-requirement aggregate) | $100 per violation, $25,000 annual cap | — |

The point isn't to memorize these numbers — it's to understand that real penalties scale by *intent* and *aggregate*, with criminal exposure for the worst cases.

### Reputational damage
The Uber 2016 breach is the canonical example: 25.6M names/emails/phones exfiltrated, breach concealed via a $100K NDA-based payment to the attackers, disclosed only in November 2017, resulting in $148M in fines (2018) and a CSO conviction (2023). Original disclosure would have been compliant. Concealment turned a breach into a crisis.

### Due care vs Due diligence
Both are about acting in good faith, but they aim differently:

| Term | Where it applies | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Due care** | Internal — what you do inside your org to stay compliant | "Are we taking reasonable steps?" |
| **Due diligence** | External — investigating and verifying third parties before/during engagement | "Have we checked the other party?" |

**Trap:** These names sound interchangeable but the exam often pairs them with internal vs external context. Internal action → due care. External investigation → due diligence.

### Attestation and acknowledgement
The executive (often the CCO) signs off that the org is in compliance in good faith. **Attestation** is the formal statement of truth; **acknowledgement** is the recognition of responsibility. The executive is personally accountable for the accuracy of the attestation.

### Automated compliance monitoring
Compliance scales badly by hand. Automation collects data from internal systems, third parties, and people, generates reports, and surfaces drift before it becomes a violation. Mature programs run continuously rather than scrambling at audit time.

## Privacy

A specific compliance domain where the *data subject* — the person the data is about — has rights. Different jurisdictions, different rights, but the structure is consistent.

### Jurisdictional layers

| Layer | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Local / state** | Vehicle registration data, medical licensing, state breach notification laws |
| **National** | HIPAA, GLBA in the US — apply nationwide |
| **International** | GDPR in the EU — applies to *anyone processing EU resident data*, regardless of where the processor is located |

### GDPR specifics
The General Data Protection Regulation is the exam's go-to international privacy example.

| GDPR concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Data subject** | "Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person" — effectively every EU resident |
| **Controller** | Entity that decides *purpose and means* of processing |
| **Processor** | Entity that processes data on behalf of the controller |
| **Right to be forgotten** | A data subject can request removal of their personal data; the website / company must comply |
| **Scope** | Covers names, addresses, photos, email, banking, online posts, location data, genetic data, and more |

**Trap:** Right to be forgotten is a *GDPR* concept first. Other regimes have followed but the exam usually associates it with GDPR specifically.

### Data roles (privacy lens)
Same roles as 5.1, but the privacy stakes are higher when these roles touch personal data:

| Role | Privacy responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Data owner** | Senior accountability for that class of data |
| **Data controller** | Decides how the data will be used — bears most regulatory liability |
| **Data processor** | Processes under controller's instructions; often an external vendor; usually bound by NDA |
| **Data custodian / steward** | Day-to-day security, accuracy, sensitivity labels, access controls |

### Data inventory
The catalog of all data the company collects and stores. For each dataset:

- Owner
- Update frequency
- Format
- Internal use (collaboration, IT security, quality)
- External sharing (legal scope, recipient, contractual basis)

Without a data inventory, you can't *prove* compliance — you don't know what you have.

## How to read scenario questions

For a 5.4 scenario, work in this order:

1. **What regulation governs the data?** Healthcare → HIPAA. Public-company financial → SOX. US financial customer → GLBA. EU resident → GDPR. Card data → PCI-DSS (contractual).
2. **Is the action internal or external?** Internal → due care. Third-party investigation → due diligence.
3. **For privacy questions,** identify the **data subject** and which **role** the actor in the question is playing (controller / processor / owner / custodian).
4. **For a non-compliance scenario,** rank consequences: fines and possible jail time first, then reputational damage, then license loss, then contract breach.
