# 5.5 — Audits and Penetration Tests

CompTIA description: *"Explain types and purposes of audits and assessments."*

Two ways to know whether your security holds up: an **audit** (paper-and-policy review) and a **penetration test** (active attack simulation). They answer different questions. Audits ask *do our controls exist and meet the bar?* Pen tests ask *would a real attacker get in despite them?* The exam tests who runs each, the framing terms (attestation, self-assessment, ROE, blind/known), the team colors, and the recon split.

---

## Audits

A structured examination of the org's infrastructure, software, policies, and procedures — verifying that controls are in place and effective. Frequently surfaces vulnerabilities *before* attackers do.

### Internal vs External

| Audit type | Run by | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal audit** | Org's own audit team / **audit committee** | Routine self-assessment; compliance status across departments |
| **External / Third-party audit** | Outside firm | Required by regulation (SOC 2, PCI-DSS, ISO 27001); independent perspective |

### Audit committee
A standing group, often at the board level, responsible for risk management and oversight of audit activity. Initiates and concludes internal audits and consumes their results.

### Self-assessment
The org's own teams evaluate their internal processes and procedures against requirements. Feeds into broader audits — auditors arrive with a head start when teams have done meaningful self-assessment first.

### Attestation
The formal **opinion of truth** about an audit's results. After completing the audit, the org or auditor *attests* to the findings — a signed statement that what's documented is what's true. This is the legal anchor: the attestation is what regulators rely on.

**Trap:** Audit ≠ attestation. The audit produces the *results*; the attestation is the *signed statement of truth* about those results. The exam may ask which step is which.

## Penetration Tests

Active attempts to breach security controls — different from a vulnerability scan, which only *identifies* weaknesses. A pen test *exploits* them.

### Why physical pen testing matters
Physical access bypasses most operating-system security. With hands on a device, an attacker can boot from removable media, modify boot files, or replace OS components. That's why production servers live in locked data centers and why physical pen tests deliberately target doors, windows, elevators, badges, mantraps, and tailgating. A physical pen test asks: can someone walk in and walk out with your security?

### Team colors

| Team | Role |
|---|---|
| **Red team** | Offense — attacks systems, looks for and exploits vulnerabilities |
| **Blue team** | Defense — identifies and blocks attacks in real time |
| **Purple team** | The integrated practice where red and blue exchange findings continuously — red finds a way in, blue learns to detect and block it, repeat |

**Trap:** Purple isn't a separate group of people in the strict sense — it's the *operating model* where red and blue work together. The exam may frame it as a team, or as a process.

### Knowledge environments
What the tester is told before the test begins shapes both the technique and what's being measured.

| Environment | What the tester knows | Also called |
|---|---|---|
| **Known environment** | Full disclosure of architecture, IPs, credentials, source code | White-box |
| **Partially known environment** | Some info given — usually to focus the test on specific systems | Grey-box |
| **Unknown environment** | No internal information — tester finds everything | Black-box, **blind test** |

**Why pick which:** Known/white-box maximizes coverage of the target. Unknown/black-box maximizes realism (mimics an outside attacker). Partially known/grey-box steers the test toward areas you specifically care about.

### Reconnaissance — Passive vs Active

Before exploiting anything, the tester gathers information. The split is whether the act of gathering leaves traces on the target's network.

| Type | What it touches | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Passive reconnaissance** | Sources outside the target's network — never queries target systems | Social media, corporate website, online forums / Reddit posts, dumpster diving, social engineering of staff, third-party vendor conversations |
| **Active reconnaissance** | Sends packets to the target — visible in logs | Ping scan, port scan, DNS query to the corporate DNS server, OS fingerprinting, version scanning |

**Trap:** Even a single ping at the target is *active*. The line is whether traffic touches the target's network, not whether the tester intends to attack yet. Passive recon by definition avoids the target's network.

### Rules of Engagement (recap from 5.3)
For any pen test — internal or vendor-contracted — the ROE scopes what's allowed: targets, schedule, IP ranges, out-of-scope systems, emergency contacts, sensitive-data handling. Without an ROE, the tester is just attacking you.

## How to read scenario questions

For a 5.5 scenario, work in this order:

1. **Is it paper review or active attack?** Controls inspection → audit. Exploit attempt → pen test.
2. **Internal or external?** Audit by your own team → internal / self-assessment. By outsider → external / third-party. Same for pen testing.
3. **For pen test framing,** identify the **environment** (known / partially known / unknown) and the **recon mode** (passive avoids your network; active touches it).
4. **For team color questions,** offense → red, defense → blue, integrated → purple.
5. **For the audit output question,** the *signed statement of truth* is the **attestation**, not the audit itself.
