# 5.2 — Risk Management

CompTIA description: *"Explain elements of the risk management process."*

Risk management is a loop: **identify → analyze → decide → track**. The exam tests four pieces of that loop: when and how you *run* an assessment (frequency), how you *measure* a risk (qualitative vs quantitative), what you *do* about it (the four strategies), and the *recovery metrics* that drive Business Impact Analysis.

---

## The risk-management loop

| Step | Question it answers | This objective's tools |
|---|---|---|
| **Identify** | What risks do we have? | Assessment frequency types — one-time, ad hoc, recurring, continuous |
| **Analyze** | How big are they? | Qualitative (high/med/low) and quantitative (ARO, AV, EF, SLE, ALE) |
| **Decide** | What do we do? | Strategies: transfer, accept, avoid, mitigate |
| **Track** | How do we know it's still managed? | Risk register, risk reporting, BIA metrics (RTO, RPO, MTTR, MTBF) |

---

## Risk Assessment frequency

Different triggers call for different assessment shapes. The frequency is part of what the exam asks.

| Type | When you use it |
|---|---|
| **One-time** | Tied to a specific project — e.g., assessing risk before an acquisition or a new system rollout |
| **Continuous / ongoing** | Built into change control — every change gets a risk look as it moves through the CCB |
| **Ad hoc** | "For this purpose only" — narrow, scoped to a single emerging threat (e.g., a new attack the CEO heard about). Committee assembles, assesses, reports, disbands. |
| **Recurring** | On a schedule — quarterly, semi-annually, or annually |
| **Mandated** | Required by a regulation — e.g., PCI-DSS requires annual assessment for orgs that store card data |

**Exam trap:** *Ad hoc* and *one-time* sound similar but the exam distinguishes them. Ad hoc is *narrowly scoped to a single new concern*; one-time is *tied to a project*. The committee for an ad hoc assessment disbands when done.

## Risk Analysis

How you put a number — or at least a color — on each risk.

### Qualitative analysis
Subjective rankings: low / medium / high, or red / yellow / green. Cheap and fast. Good for sweeping the landscape and prioritizing where to spend deeper analysis effort.

Common dimensions evaluated qualitatively:

| Dimension | What it captures |
|---|---|
| **Impact** | How bad if it happens |
| **Annualized rate of occurrence (ARO)** | How often it's likely to happen per year |
| **Cost of controls** | What mitigating it would cost |
| **Overall risk** | Composite score |

### Quantitative analysis
Real dollar values via a chain of variables.

| Variable | Means | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **AV** — Asset Value | What the asset is worth (replacement + lost sales + fines + other) | given |
| **EF** — Exposure Factor | % of the asset lost in a single event (0.0 to 1.0) | given |
| **ARO** — Annualized Rate of Occurrence | How many times per year you expect it | given |
| **SLE** — Single Loss Expectancy | Loss per single event | **SLE = AV × EF** |
| **ALE** — Annualized Loss Expectancy | Expected loss per year | **ALE = SLE × ARO** |

**Worked example:** Laptop theft. AV = $1,000. EF = 1.0 (the whole laptop is gone). SLE = $1,000 × 1.0 = $1,000. If you expect 7 stolen per year, ARO = 7, so ALE = $1,000 × 7 = $7,000.

**Exam trap:** SLE and ALE are the two most commonly tested. Memorize **SLE = AV × EF** and **ALE = SLE × ARO**. The exam may give you partial variables and require you to derive the rest.

### Impact categories
Beyond dollars, evaluate every risk for impact on:

| Category | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Life** | Top priority — you can replace assets, not people |
| **Property** | Buildings, equipment, infrastructure |
| **Safety** | Risk of injury to people during the event |
| **Finance** | The dollar exposure (where quantitative analysis goes) |

### Likelihood vs Probability
Used interchangeably in casual talk; the exam can be precise:

- **Likelihood** — qualitative (rare, possible, almost certain).
- **Probability** — quantitative (a statistic or measurement, often from historical data).

### Risk appetite vs Risk tolerance
Closely related but distinct:

| Term | What it captures |
|---|---|
| **Risk appetite** | The level of risk the org *prefers* to accept. Can be expressed as posture: **conservative · neutral · expansionary**. |
| **Risk tolerance** | The variance around appetite the org *will tolerate* before acting. Usually wider than appetite. |

**Analogy from the transcript:** The speed limit is 55 mph — that's law enforcement's *appetite*. They typically don't ticket until 60–65 — that's their *tolerance*. Tolerance can shift situationally (bad weather → lower tolerance).

### Risk register
The living document tracking every risk on a project or in an org. Each row has:

- **Key risk indicator (KRI)** — the specific risk (e.g., "project scope is not well defined")
- **Risk owner** — who manages it
- **Risk threshold** — the trigger point where action is required
- Mitigation plan / options

## Risk Management Strategies

Once you've measured a risk, you have to do something with it. Four primary strategies, plus the exception/exemption nuance.

| Strategy | What you do | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Transfer** | Shift the risk to a third party | Buy cyber-insurance |
| **Accept** | Decide to live with the risk | The cost of mitigation exceeds the expected loss |
| **Avoid** | Eliminate the source of the risk | Don't launch the risky feature; decommission the legacy service |
| **Mitigate** | Reduce the risk (lower impact, lower ARO, or both) | Install a next-gen firewall to lower internet-borne risk |

**Accept ≠ Ignore.** Accepted risk is *documented and tracked* — you've decided not to act, but you know it's there. Ignored risk isn't even on the register.

### Exemption vs Exception
Two flavors of accepted risk that the exam likes to distinguish:

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Exemption** | A device or system is permanently outside a policy's scope | A vendor-locked manufacturing PC running Windows that the vendor forbids patching — exempted from monthly patch policy (and kept off the network) |
| **Exception** | A temporary deviation from a policy for cause | A patch breaks a critical app, so the 3-day patching window is exceptioned to allow time to fix the conflict |

**Trap:** *Exemption is structural, exception is temporary.* The exam may describe one and offer both as options.

### Risk reporting
The living document — separate from the register — that surfaces critical and emerging risks to upper management who make budget decisions.

## Business Impact Analysis

BIA quantifies how a disruption hits the business so recovery planning has real numbers to optimize against. Four time/durability metrics dominate the exam.

| Metric | What it measures | Mnemonic |
|---|---|---|
| **RTO** — Recovery Time Objective | How long it takes to get back up and running | *Time* to recovery |
| **RPO** — Recovery Point Objective | How much data loss is acceptable (point in time you must recover to) | *Point* to recover to |
| **MTTR** — Mean Time To Repair / Restore | Average time to fix a problem once it occurs | How long fixes *take* |
| **MTBF** — Mean Time Between Failures | Average time a system runs between outages | How long things *last* |

### RTO vs RPO — the most-confused pair

- **RTO** is a *duration looking forward from the outage*. "We need the database back online within 4 hours."
- **RPO** is a *point in time looking back from the outage*. "We can tolerate losing at most 1 hour of transactions."

Set together they bound the recovery plan. RPO drives backup frequency; RTO drives restoration tooling and standby capacity.

### MTTR vs MTBF
Both are reliability metrics. MTBF is *uptime between events* — usually vendor-stated for new gear, or calculated from your own history (total uptime ÷ number of failures). MTTR is *repair duration*. You shrink MTTR by pre-staging spares or by contracting fast vendor SLAs.

**Exam trap:** MTBF includes the word "between failures" — it's the *gap*, not the *repair time*. MTTR is repair time. Don't mix them.

## How to read scenario questions

For a 5.2 scenario, work in this order:

1. **Are you analyzing or deciding?** Analysis questions want a metric (SLE, ALE, qualitative score). Decision questions want a strategy (transfer / accept / avoid / mitigate).
2. **For quantitative questions,** identify which variable is given and which is asked. Map to SLE = AV × EF and ALE = SLE × ARO.
3. **For decisions,** check the language: "shift to a third party" → transfer; "live with it" → accept; "stop doing the thing" → avoid; "reduce the impact" → mitigate.
4. **For accepted risk,** is it structural and permanent (**exemption**) or temporary for cause (**exception**)?
5. **For BIA scenarios,** is the question about duration to recovery (**RTO**) or about how much data loss is OK (**RPO**)?
