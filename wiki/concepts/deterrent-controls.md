# Deterrent Controls

**One-line:** Controls that **discourage** an attacker without physically preventing the attack. The attack remains possible; the calculus changes.

## Examples across categories

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Technical | Login banner / splash screen warning about unauthorized access and monitoring |
| Managerial | Stated policy of demotion or dismissal for unauthorized access |
| Operational | Front reception desk visibly greeting / logging visitors |
| Physical | "Beware of dog," CCTV-in-use, or "warning: trespassers will be prosecuted" signs |

## Why deterrents matter even though they don't block

Most real attackers (insider or external opportunist) are doing a cost-benefit. A deterrent raises the perceived cost — legal consequences, social cost, embarrassment — enough to make them pick a different target. Useless against a determined attacker; quite effective against casual ones.

## Exam traps

- **"Discourages" vs "prevents."** A warning sign deters; a fence prevents. If the question uses words like "may think twice," "warns," or "informs of consequences," it's deterrent.
- **A login banner that just warns is deterrent. A login screen that requires a password is preventive.** Same screen, different controls.
- **Deterrents often work in tandem with preventives.** Don't overthink — if the question describes both, identify both.
